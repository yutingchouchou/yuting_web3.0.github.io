Introduction

Before participating in today's informal meeting, the only thing I knew related to IBS was IPS stem cells.

--- Y.T. Chou

IntraBeam Scattering (IBS) occurs naturally due to the Coulomb force between electrons in a bunch. In accelerator physics, the phase space with coordinates \(x, y, z\) and corresponding momenta \(p_x, p_y, p_z\) is a set of suitable parameters to describe the behavior of a bunch of electrons. The basic assumption is that all the electrons interact with each other via conservative forces, so Liouville's theorem works well to predict the position-momentum distribution of the electron beam. However, the emission of the electron beam after passing through the undulator causes energy loss, and collisions between electrons couple all the parameters in phase space. As a result, the beam size and the number of particles (i.e., Touschek effect) change, achieving a new equilibrium beam emittance. The luminosity is limited by this process.

Interestingly, the IBS rate has a \(\frac{1}{\gamma^4}\) dependence, implying that the larger the \(\frac{E}{mc^2}\) value, the more negligible the effect is (i.e., the faster the bunch is, the time dilation gives less time to electron interact together).

Today's meeting will go through the fundamental derivation via the Landau collision integral (CI), which was first developed to deal with plasma in 1936 (i.e., non-equilibrium system, Boltzmann equation, with an extra interaction term).

Landau Collision Integral

Let's consider the total time derivative of the particle distribution \(f(\vec{v}, \vec{p}, t)\) and the external force on the left-hand side, then considering the friction force \(\vec{a}(\vec{v})\),

\[ \frac{df(\vec{r}, \vec{p}, t)}{dt}=\frac{\partial f}{\partial t} + \vec{v} \cdot \nabla_{\vec{r}} f+\frac{d\vec{p}}{dt}\frac{\partial f}{\partial\vec{p}} \]

The first two terms on the right hand side is the common result because of the space time parameter for the distribution \(f\), and the third term is, obviously, coming from the momentum dependent in the phase space. In the system, the momentum change is caused by Lorentz force as shown below:

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial t} + \vec{v} \cdot \nabla_{\vec{r}} f + e\left(\vec{E} + \frac{\vec{v}}{c} \times \vec{B}\right) \cdot \nabla_{\vec{p}} f = L \frac{\partial}{\partial v_i} \left( -a_i f + \frac{1}{2} D_{ij} \frac{\partial f}{\partial v_j} \right) \]

The right-hand side with the constant value \(L = \frac{4\pi n_e e^4}{m^2} \Lambda\), and the friction force \(\vec{a}(\vec{v})\) and the interaction coefficient \(D_{ij}\) could be shown as follows:

\[ \vec{a}(\vec{v}) = -\int f_e(\vec{v'}) \frac{\vec{u}}{u^3} d^3 \vec{v'} \]

\[ D_{ij}(\vec{v}) = \int f(\vec{v'}) \frac{u^2 \delta_{ij} - u_i u_j}{|\vec{u}|^3} d^3 \vec{v'} \]

The next step is to apply the small-angle approximation, and the whole scattering picture could be viewed as a field interaction picture.

Small Angle Approximation

Consider the classical scattering picture with two particles with impact parameter \(b\) and the scattering angle \(\theta\). The small angle approximation could be equivalent to the high energy approximation (J.D. Bjorken 1982) by carefully considering the exchange and ratio between kinetic energy and potential energy.

The focus on "angle" or "energy exchange" implies the focus on "field distribution" or "particles' position".
